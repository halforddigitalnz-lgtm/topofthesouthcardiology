For patients

Wondering if you need
specialist heart care?

You don't need to figure this out alone. Here's what cardiologists look for, how referrals work, and what to expect if you go ahead.

Start with your GP

We can't take self-referrals — all patients need a GP or specialist referral. If you're concerned about your heart, book an appointment with your GP and tell them your symptoms. They'll decide whether a referral is warranted and arrange it for you.

Common reasons to see a cardiologist

Symptoms that may warrant specialist input

These are some of the most common reasons GPs refer patients to us. This list isn't exhaustive — if you're concerned about your heart for any reason, speak to your GP.

Having one of these symptoms doesn't mean there's something seriously wrong — but it does mean it's worth having it properly assessed.

  • Chest pain or tightness Particularly with exertion or that radiates to the arm, jaw, or back.
  • Palpitations or irregular heartbeat Fluttering, racing, or a sensation of skipped beats — particularly if frequent or associated with dizziness.
  • Breathlessness Unexplained shortness of breath with activity or at rest, or that has changed recently.
  • Blackouts or fainting Unexplained syncope, near-fainting, or persistent dizziness may have a cardiac cause.
  • High blood pressure Resistant or newly elevated blood pressure, particularly if other risk factors are present.
  • Significantly elevated cholesterol Particularly in younger patients or those with a strong family history of heart disease.
  • Family history of heart disease Early heart attack, sudden cardiac death, or inherited cardiac conditions in close relatives.
  • Occupational medical clearance Pilots, commercial drivers, and others who require cardiac assessment for fitness-to-fly or fitness-to-drive.
How it works

Getting a referral

The process is simpler than many people expect. Here's what typically happens.

1

Book an appointment with your GP

Tell your GP about your symptoms — be as specific as you can about when they happen, how long they last, and anything that makes them better or worse.

2

Your GP decides whether to refer

If your GP thinks a cardiology assessment is appropriate, they'll write a referral letter and send it to us. They may also do some initial tests first (like an ECG or blood tests).

3

We review the referral and contact you

We'll review your referral and contact you directly to arrange an appointment. You don't need to follow up — we'll be in touch.

4

Your appointment

You'll see one of our cardiologists in Nelson or Marlborough. After your assessment, they'll explain what they found and what — if anything — needs to happen next.

Funding & cost

What does it cost?

Costs vary depending on whether you have health insurance, self-fund, or are publicly referred. Here's how each pathway works.

Health insurance

Most private health insurers cover specialist cardiology consultations and standard investigations. We are a Southern Cross Affiliated Provider — members usually don't need pre-authorisation for standard consultations.

Check your policy and confirm cover with your insurer before your appointment.

Self-funding

Many patients self-fund for reassurance investigations — a consultation, echocardiogram, or calcium score. We'll tell you the expected cost upfront so there are no surprises.

A GP referral is still required even if you're self-funding.

Public funding

Some investigations are publicly funded through Nelson Marlborough Health. Your GP will know whether your situation may qualify and can advise on the appropriate pathway.

Wait times for publicly funded services vary.

Ready to take the next step?

Start by making an appointment with your GP and mentioning your cardiac concerns. They'll guide you from there.
