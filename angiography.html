Procedures

What our angiography suite can do

Our catheterisation laboratory handles both diagnostic procedures — finding the problem — and interventional procedures — treating it.

Diagnostic

Coronary Angiogram

A dye injected into the coronary arteries under X-ray imaging allows us to see blockages or narrowings that may be restricting blood flow to the heart muscle. Usually performed via a small incision at the wrist (radial access).

Left Heart Catheterisation

Assessment of pressures within the heart chambers and large vessels, helping to characterise valve disease, heart muscle function, and certain congenital conditions.

Intracoronary Physiological Assessment (FFR / iFR)

Fractional flow reserve (FFR) and instantaneous wave-free ratio (iFR) measurements help determine whether a blockage is actually causing reduced blood flow to the heart — helping decide whether a stent is truly needed. Note: FFR requires avoiding caffeine before the procedure.

Intravascular Ultrasound (IVUS)

A miniature ultrasound probe on a catheter provides detailed images from inside the coronary artery — showing exactly how much plaque is present, its composition, and helping guide precise stent placement.

Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT)

Uses light rather than ultrasound to achieve extremely high-resolution images of the inside of coronary arteries — providing even finer detail than IVUS. Used to optimise stent deployment and assess artery wall structure.

Interventional / Treatment

Percutaneous Coronary Intervention (PCI / Stenting)

A tiny balloon is inflated inside a narrowed coronary artery, followed by the placement of a metal mesh stent to hold the artery open and restore blood flow. This is the treatment for many types of coronary artery disease and some heart attacks.

Pacemaker & Defibrillator Implantation

Implantation of permanent pacemakers (to treat slow or irregular heart rhythms) and implantable cardioverter-defibrillators (ICDs) to protect against life-threatening fast rhythms.

Electrophysiology & Ablation

Mapping of abnormal electrical pathways causing heart rhythm problems, and treatment by ablation — using heat or cold energy to scar and correct the abnormal tissue.

What to expect

Your procedure, step by step

Most procedures are day cases — you'll be home the same day.

1

Pre-procedure preparation

You'll receive specific instructions about fasting and medications. A pre-admission call from our nursing team will walk you through what to expect and answer any questions.

2

Arrival and preparation

You'll arrive at the clinic, change into a gown, and have an IV line placed. Your cardiologist will go through the procedure with you and address any last-minute concerns before you sign a consent form.

3

The procedure

Most diagnostic procedures take 30–60 minutes. You'll be awake throughout, with local anaesthetic at the access site. You may be sedated to keep you comfortable. Our team will talk you through what's happening.

4

Recovery and results

You'll rest in our recovery area for a few hours while we monitor you. Your cardiologist will come and explain what was found and what it means. For most day cases, you'll be home by afternoon.

5

Follow-up

We'll arrange a follow-up appointment to review your progress, adjust medications if needed, and answer any questions once you've had time to digest the results. A report will also be sent to your GP.

Preparing for your procedure

Practical information

Fasting

You'll typically be asked to fast from midnight the night before, or from 6 hours before your procedure time. Water and essential medications are usually allowed — your specific instructions will be confirmed when your appointment is booked.

Medications

Do not stop any medications unless specifically instructed by our team. Some blood-thinning medications may need to be paused. We'll review this with you individually before your procedure.

Bring someone

Please arrange for someone to drive you home — you will not be able to drive after sedation. It is also a good idea to have someone with you when you hear the results, as there can be a lot to take in.

What to wear

Loose, comfortable clothing works best. You'll change into a hospital gown for the procedure. Leave jewellery and valuables at home.

Cost & insurance

Many angiographic procedures are covered by health insurance. We are a Southern Cross Affiliated Provider. For self-funded patients, costs will be discussed before your procedure so there are no surprises.

Questions?

Our team is happy to talk through any concerns before your procedure. Email us at clinicalsupport@topofthesouthcardiology.co.nz — we'd much rather you arrive feeling informed and calm than arrive feeling anxious.
